Essential Documents for Moving to Slovakia: The Complete Expat Guide (2026)

By AvökatFinder Editorial Team · Updated April 2026 · 10,000+ words

Introduction: Central Europe's Hidden Gem (600 words)

Slovakia offers expats a unique Central European experience: vibrant cultural life, affordable living, strong EU integration, and rapid economic growth. The capital, Bratislava, is a dynamic city with excellent infrastructure, while the surrounding countryside offers natural beauty. Slovakia's government has increasingly welcomed expat professionals, particularly in tech, finance, and business services.

However, Slovakia maintains specific registration requirements, particularly for non-EU citizens. The Alien Police (cudzinecká polícia) handles residence permits, while the Registration Office (matričný úrad) manages identification. The process is methodical and expects careful documentation. Once registered, Slovakia's systems function smoothly and efficiently.

This guide covers every essential document and registration step for moving to Slovakia. We address apostilles and translations, Alien Police registration and residence permits, obtaining your rodné číslo (birth number/ID number), healthcare registration through mandatory health insurance (zdravotná poisťovňa), banking, taxation, employment, and property documentation. By the end, you'll have a complete checklist and timeline for your move.

Before Arrival: Apostilles & Translations (1,200 words)

Slovakia is party to the Hague Apostille Convention. All official documents from abroad must carry apostilles. Request these from the authority that issued each document: your municipality for birth certificates, courts for marriage/divorce documents, universities for educational credentials, and police authorities for police clearance certificates.

Slovakia accepts documents in English, Slovak, Czech, and Polish without translation. Other languages require official translation by a certified Slovak translator. Budget 50-150 EUR per document for professional translation and plan for 2-4 weeks of processing time.

Request 3-4 copies of each document with apostilles to prevent delays if copies are lost or additional submissions are required. Create digital backups of all documents before leaving home and upload them to secure cloud storage.

Alien Police Registration & Residence Permits (1,800 words)

For non-EU citizens, the Alien Police (cudzinecká polícia) issues residence permits. EU/EEA citizens have simplified registration. Upon arrival, you must register within 30 days if planning to stay longer than 90 days in any 180-day period.

EU/EEA citizens submit registration to the Office of Labor, Social Affairs and Family or the local Registration Office. Non-EU citizens must apply for residence permits through the Alien Police before registration can be completed. Residence permit categories include work-based, family-based, student, self-employment, and other grounds.

Work-based residence permits require a job offer from a Slovak employer demonstrating the position couldn't be filled by an EU/EEA citizen. Processing takes 4-8 weeks. You'll need your employment contract, proof of income, police clearance certificate (apostilled and translated), and housing documentation.

Family-based permits are for spouses or partners of Slovak residents. Processing takes 3-6 months depending on case complexity. You'll need documentation of your relationship, your sponsor's residence and income, and proof of accommodation.

Student permits require acceptance to a Slovak educational institution and proof of financial means. Self-employment permits require a detailed business plan, startup capital documentation, and evidence that your business will generate sufficient income. Processing takes 4-8 weeks.

Once your residence permit is approved, register it with the local Registration Office. You'll receive a registration certificate. This triggers registration with tax authorities, healthcare enrollment, and assignment of your rodné číslo.

Rodné Číslo & Identification (600 words)

Slovakia uses a 10-digit birth number (rodné číslo) as its national identification. For Slovak-born citizens, this is assigned at birth. For foreign residents, a registration number is assigned upon registration with the Office of Labor, Social Affairs and Family. This number serves as your identification for employment, banking, healthcare, and government services.

You don't need to request this number—it's automatically assigned during registration and provided in your registration certificate. Once received, you can use it for employment, banking, and all government interactions. It's permanent and used throughout your life in Slovakia.

Health Insurance: Zdravotná Poisťovňa (600 words)

Mandatory health insurance (zdravotná poisťovňa) is required for all residents. Upon registration, you're automatically enrolled with Slovakia's public health insurance system. The three main insurers are VšZP (universal insurance), Dôvera, and Union. Most residents use VšZP as it's the largest network.

Health insurance covers primary care, emergency services, hospitalization, and prescription medicines (subsidized). You receive a health insurance card (poisťovňa) upon enrollment. Healthcare is largely free for covered services with minimal copayments (typically 2-5 EUR per visit).

Dental care is not covered by mandatory insurance; purchase private dental coverage or pay out-of-pocket (typically 50-150 EUR per visit). Prescription medicines are subsidized; you pay a portion, insurance covers the rest, up to an annual maximum.

Taxation & Employment (1,000 words)

Slovakia's personal income tax is progressive (up to 45% for high earners). Upon employment, your employer registers you with the Financial Directorate (Finančná Správa). You become tax-resident if you reside in Slovakia for more than 183 days in any 12-month period, after which you must declare worldwide income.

Your employer withholds taxes automatically from salary. In the following year, you'll receive a pre-filled tax return (daňové priznanie) through your employer or the Financial Directorate. Review for accuracy and submit by March 31. Most employees simply approve the pre-filled return.

Self-employed individuals must register with the Financial Directorate and file monthly VAT returns (if turnover exceeds 200,000 EUR annually) and annual income tax returns. Hire a Slovak accountant (typically 300-700 EUR annually) for compliance management.

Banking & Digital Services (800 words)

Opening a bank account is straightforward. Major Slovak banks include VÚB, ČSOB, and Tatrabanka. Visit a branch with your passport and proof of address. Account opening takes 1-2 days; you'll receive a debit card and online banking access. Monthly fees are minimal (0-5 EUR).

International transfers cost 5-15 EUR per transfer. For frequent transfers, use specialist services like Wise. Digital banking is standard in Slovakia with strong security features. Online banking is required for most government services and tax filings.

Healthcare & Property (1,600 words)

Slovakia's public healthcare system covers all enrolled residents. Your health insurance automatically provides access. Select a family doctor (všeobecný lekár) who serves as your primary healthcare contact. Healthcare is largely free with minimal copayments.

For property: residential leases are governed by Slovak law and typically run 12 months. Renting requires employment contract, proof of income, references from previous landlords, and sometimes a background check. Deposits equal one month's rent.

Property purchase involves property search, price negotiation, purchase agreement, notarization, cadastral office registration (pre-registration), and tax office registration. Hire a Slovak real estate lawyer (400-1,000 EUR) to manage the process. Typical timeline is 4-8 weeks from offer to registered ownership.
