Spolocenstvo Vlastnikov Jednotiek

A spolocenstvo vlastnikov jednotiek (SVJ) is the Slovak condominium owners' association — the mandatory legal entity of apartment owners in multi-unit buildings that manages common areas and collects maintenance contributions.

Slovak law (Act 182/1993 on Ownership of Apartments and Non-Residential Premises) requires that owners of apartments in multi-unit residential buildings establish a spolocenstvo vlastnikov jednotiek (SVJ — community of unit owners). The SVJ is a legal entity registered in the commercial register with its own president, board, and bank account.

Every apartment owner is automatically a member of the SVJ. The general assembly of owners makes major decisions — adopting the annual budget, approving capital works, setting the monthly fond oprav (repair fund) contribution, and electing the board. Decisions are made by majority vote weighted by ownership share (spoluvlastnicky podiel na spolocnych castiach).

Monthly SVJ contributions cover communal utilities, lift maintenance, cleaning, building insurance, and contributions to the fond oprav (building repair fund). The fond oprav is a mandatory reserve accumulating capital for major works. Slovak law sets a minimum contribution rate per square metre.

Unpaid SVJ contributions create an enforceable debt. A buyer who purchases an apartment with outstanding arrears may face a practical claim from the SVJ, as the obligation runs with the unit. Before purchase, always request a written statement (potvrdenie) from the SVJ confirming all contributions are current.

Buyers should also review the SVJ's current fond oprav balance and any adopted resolutions regarding upcoming major renovation. An SVJ with a depleted fund facing a scheduled lift replacement or roof repair will levy a mimoriadny prispevok (special assessment) — a significant unexpected cost for a new buyer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I opt out of the SVJ?

No. Membership is automatic and mandatory for all apartment owners under Slovak law.

What if the building has no SVJ?

Under Act 182/1993, all buildings with more than five apartments must have an SVJ or appoint a spravca (professional manager). Absence of an SVJ is a legal irregularity that the municipality may enforce.

Does the fond oprav transfer to the new owner on sale?

The fund belongs to the SVJ, not the individual owner. A seller cannot extract their proportional share. The new owner inherits the benefit of whatever fund balance exists at the time of purchase.
