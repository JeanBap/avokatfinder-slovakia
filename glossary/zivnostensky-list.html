Živnostenský list (Trade Licence)

The official Slovak trade licence issued by the Živnostenský úrad authorising individuals to conduct self-employed business activities (živnosť) in Slovakia.

The živnostenský list (trade licence) is the authorisation document issued by the Živnostenský úrad (Trade Licensing Authority) that permits an individual to operate a živnosť — a registered self-employment or small business activity in Slovakia. Nearly all freelance, consulting, and small business activities in Slovakia require a live živnostenský list. Expatriates from EU countries have the same right as Slovak citizens to obtain a živnostenský list. Non-EU nationals may require a residence permit with business authorisation before they can apply.

Slovak law classifies živnosti into three categories. Craft trades (remeselné živnosti) such as electrical work, plumbing, or construction require proof of professional qualification or apprenticeship. Regulated trades (viazané živnosti) such as accounting, travel agency operation, or certain healthcare services require specific education or professional licences. Free trades (voľné živnosti) covering most consulting, IT, e-commerce, and general business activities require no specific qualification beyond legal capacity. Most expats launching service businesses operate under free trade classification.

Applying for a živnostenský list is handled through the Central Registration Point (Centrálne kontaktné miesto, or CKM) located at district offices (okresné úrady) throughout Slovakia. Applications can now be submitted online via slovensko.sk using a qualified electronic signature. The process requires completing an application form, providing a copy of your ID or passport, declaring your Slovak address, and paying a fee of €5 for each free trade activity. Processing typically takes 3 business days for free trades and up to 30 days for regulated categories.

Operating without a valid živnostenský list when one is required constitutes an administrative offence (priestupok) in Slovakia. Penalties include fines and potential suspension of business activities. All holders of a živnostenský list are required to register with the Slovak Tax Authority (Finančná správa) for income tax purposes, and typically with the Social Insurance Company (Sociálna poisťovňa) and health insurance company. Even if your income is below the taxable threshold, registration obligations may still apply. Consulting a Slovak accountant or tax adviser when setting up is strongly recommended.

Slovak living with an active živnostenský list are often referred to colloquially as živnostníci. Many expats find the živnostník status useful when starting out in Slovakia as it allows them to invoice clients, work with multiple clients, and benefit from certain flat-rate expense deductions. The standard flat-rate expense deduction is 60% of gross income, with a cap, which can significantly reduce taxable income. However, živnostníci are responsible for their own social and health insurance contributions, which are mandatory from the first year of operation. Planning for these quarterly and annual obligations is essential for financial health.

Key Facts

  • Required for most self-employed and freelance activities in Slovakia
  • Three categories: free (voľné), craft (remeselné), and regulated (viazané) trades
  • Applications submitted at district offices or online via slovensko.sk
  • Processing takes 3 business days for free trades
  • Holders must register with tax, social, and health insurance authorities

⚠ Common Mistake

Many new živnostníci underestimate their social and health insurance contribution obligations in the first year. In the launch year, contributions are typically based on a minimum base, but from the second year they are recalculated based on actual income. Expats sometimes miss the deadline to report their previous year income to Sociálna poisťovňa, resulting in penalties. Set calendar reminders for the June deadline each year to submit your annual tax return and notify insurance institutions.

💡 Practical Tip

If you plan to work with EU clients and invoice VAT-free under the EU reverse charge mechanism, register for VAT (DPH) proactively even if your turnover is below the €49,790 threshold. Cross-border service providers benefit from having a VAT number when invoicing foreign clients. Consult a Slovak accountant before your first year of trading to set up proper bookkeeping and understand your quarterly advance payment (preddavok) obligations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can an EU citizen get a živnostenský list in Slovakia?

Yes. EU citizens have the same rights as Slovak nationals to obtain a živnostenský list. You need a valid EU ID or passport, a Slovak address (can be a rented apartment), and the application fee. You do not need a separate work permit or residence permit as an EU citizen.

What is the difference between a živnostenský list and an s.r.o.?

A živnostenský list (živnosť) gives you personal self-employment status — you operate as an individual with unlimited liability. An s.r.o. (Spoločnosť s ručením obmedzeným) is a limited liability company — a separate legal entity that limits your personal liability. Živnosť is simpler and cheaper to set up; s.r.o. offers better liability protection for larger operations.

How much does it cost to get a živnostenský list?

The fee is €5 per free trade activity. Craft and regulated trades have higher fees (€15 per activity). If you are applying online with a qualified electronic signature via slovensko.sk, fees may be reduced. Additional costs include accounting and tax registration setup, which typically runs €100-300 if you hire an accountant to help.
