Kataster nehnuteľností (Land Registry)

The official Slovak land register maintained by the Geodesy, Cartography and Cadastre Authority, recording ownership rights and legal charges on all real property.

The kataster nehnuteľností (land registry or cadastre) is Slovakia's official register of real property, maintained by the Úrad geodézie, kartografie a katastra Slovenskej republiky (Geodesy, Cartography and Cadastre Authority). It records ownership, mortgages, easements, pre-emption rights, and other legal relationships involving land and buildings. Before purchasing any real estate in Slovakia, reviewing the relevant cadastral entry (list vlastníctva, or LV) is a fundamental legal step. The LV confirms who legally owns the property and what charges or encumbrances are registered against it.

The list vlastníctva (ownership sheet) is divided into four parts. Part A describes the property—its cadastral district, parcel number, area, and type (e.g. residential building, agricultural land). Part B identifies the current owner(s) and their ownership shares. Part C records burdens on the property including mortgages (záložné právo), easements (vecné bremeno), and pre-emption rights (predkupné právo). Part D contains notes and annotations about pending changes or disputes. Reviewing all four parts is essential for any buyer.

Accessing the Slovak cadastre has become straightforward with the online portal (katasterportal.sk). You can search by cadastral district and parcel number to view ownership and burden information. Certified extracts (overený výpis) are available for a fee from local cadastre offices or authorised submission agents (notári, advokáti, and Slovenská pošta). Certified extracts are typically required for mortgage applications and official transactions. Uncertified online extracts are useful for initial due diligence but may not be accepted for formal legal proceedings.

Property transfers in Slovakia must be registered with the cadastre to be legally effective. Once a purchase agreement (kúpna zmluva) is signed, your lawyer (advokát) or notary (notár) submits a registration application (návrh na vklad) along with the contract and supporting documents. The standard registration period is 30 days from application receipt. An expedited procedure (urýchlená procedúra) is available for double the standard fee, typically processing within a few days. Until your ownership is registered, the previous owner remains the legal owner even if you have paid in full.

Slovakia imposes specific rules on agricultural and forestry land transactions. Non-residents and foreign entities face significant restrictions on purchasing such land. Even Slovak buyers must comply with pre-emption rights held by neighbouring landowners and local municipalities when purchasing agricultural parcels. Your advokát must verify whether pre-emption rights apply and ensure proper waivers are obtained before the transaction closes. Violations of pre-emption rights can render a purchase void, leaving the buyer without recourse against the seller in some circumstances.

Key Facts

  • Maintained by Geodesy, Cartography and Cadastre Authority (ÚGKK SR)
  • List vlastníctva (LV) divided into parts A (property), B (owner), C (burdens), D (notes)
  • Online search available at katasterportal.sk
  • Registration takes 30 days (expedited available at higher fee)
  • Agricultural land purchases restricted for non-residents and foreign entities

⚠ Common Mistake

Buyers often check only Part B of the ownership sheet (confirming the seller is the owner) without reviewing Part C (burdens). Mortgages (záložné právo) registered in Part C can significantly complicate a purchase—if not properly discharged before or at closing, the buyer may inherit the debt obligation. Also, many buyers fail to check Part D annotations, which may reveal pending court disputes or tax liens not yet fully registered.

💡 Practical Tip

Request a fresh cadastre extract on the day of signing, as new entries can appear at any time. When purchasing from a developer, ask to see the cadastral map (katastrálna mapa) to verify the exact boundary of what you are buying. For apartment purchases, confirm the specific basement storage unit, parking space, and shared facilities included in your ownership share are correctly listed in the cadastral documents.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I search the Slovak cadastre online?

Visit katasterportal.sk and search by cadastral district (katastrálne územie) and parcel number (číslo parcely) or owner name. The portal provides unofficial extracts free of charge. For certified official extracts, visit a cadastre office, use a notary or advokát, or submit an authenticated request through authorised channels.

What is a vecné bremeno and should I be concerned about it?

A vecné bremeno is an easement — a right attached to the property rather than to a person. Common examples include rights of way, utility access routes, and rights to use a water source. Easements pass with the property when it is sold. Review each easement carefully with your lawyer to understand whether it affects your intended use of the property.

How long does property registration take in Slovakia?

Standard registration (vklad) takes up to 30 days from the date the application is received. An expedited procedure (urýchlená) is available for twice the standard administrative fee and is typically processed within a few working days. Your advokát or notár will advise on which procedure is appropriate for your transaction.
